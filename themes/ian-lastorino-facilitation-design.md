# Facilitation design and participant engagement

## The growth area

Ian is an L&D professional whose sessions are perceived as valuable but can fail to generate
live engagement. He notices the gap (flat room, stilted when he calls on people) but hasn't
yet located the design dimension underneath it: whether the session was architected for
active participation or whether participation was added onto a delivery-mode structure.

The difference matters because calling on people in a delivery session creates performative
participation — it interrupts a one-way flow rather than structuring a two-way exchange.
The coach's first diagnostic: was this session built for them to *do* something, or built
to *receive* something?

## Observations

- **2026-06-18 — [training facilitation: the engagement gap](../sessions/ian-lastorino/2026-06-18-training-facilitation-gap.md)** — Ian describes a session where the audience was flat and calling on people felt stilted. Post-session, attendees said it was valuable. Gap between in-room responsiveness and perceived value is unaccounted for. Coach's diagnostic question (design-for-participation vs. delivery-with-participation-layered-on) is in flight — Ian's answer will tell whether the gap is structural (session format) or execution (how he called on people).
- **2026-06-18 — [facilitation: reading participant silence](../sessions/ian-lastorino/2026-06-18-facilitation-reading-silence.md)** — Coach's diagnostic (design vs. delivery mode) answered implicitly: "How would I know, they didn't speak?" — the lack of signal to read is itself evidence that the session didn't generate it. Ian frames silence as what participants chose rather than what the design didn't invite. Coach reframes: people did respond when called on (stilted counts). New diagnostic introduced: was the stilted response fumbling (nothing to say) or short and guarded (something there, not offering it)? Ian has not yet answered.
- **2026-06-18 — [facilitation participation diagnostic](../sessions/ian-lastorino/2026-06-18-facilitation-participation-diagnostic.md)** — Ian confirms the session was designed for participation: open-ended activities tied to participants' own AI workflow work, not generic filler. Design is ruled out as the cause. New diagnostic in flight: was the silence because participants didn't have an answer ready (cognitive/readiness gap) or because they had one and weren't putting it out (social/psychological safety gap)? These two causes point to very different interventions.
- **2026-06-18 — [facilitation format choice](../sessions/ian-lastorino/2026-06-18-facilitation-format-choice.md)** — Ian pushes back on the implicit small-groups frame: he knows small groups loosen things up, but this was a "collaborative session, not that type of training," and the whole-room format was intentional. Coach's diagnostic shifts: what was whole-room supposed to protect that small groups would have cost? Ian draws a meaningful distinction between session types (training vs. collaborative) and treats that taxonomy as load-bearing for format decisions. Whether that taxonomy maps reliably onto what actually generates participation is the open question — Ian hasn't yet named what whole-room was preserving.
- **2026-06-18 — [facilitation: participants had content, wouldn't volunteer it](../sessions/ian-lastorino/2026-06-18-facilitation-participants-had-plenty.md)** — Ian confirms participants had plenty to say; the barrier was volunteering it, not having it. Cognitive/readiness gap ruled out; social gap confirmed. Coach now probing whether activities used pairs/small groups before whole-room share, or went straight to cold-call whole-room questioning — the answer determines whether the design gave participants a low-stakes on-ramp before being asked to perform for the room.
