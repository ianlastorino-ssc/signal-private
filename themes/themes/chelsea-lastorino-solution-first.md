# Solution-first entry in difficult conversations

## What it looks like
Chelsea steps into emotionally charged situations holding a predetermined outcome or resource. She goes in to *convince*, to *explain*, to *offer* — before she's learned what the other person is actually up against. When the person pushes back, the instinct is to offer the next available thing rather than pause and ask what's driving the resistance.

## The structure of the blindspot
Two moves in one session showed the same underlying shape:

1. **The "convince" framing** — Chelsea's stated goal going in was to convince the fiancée that discharge was appropriate. That's an outcome-led entry. It makes curiosity harder because the conclusion is already loaded.

2. **The transportation offer** — When the fiancée named caregiver burden (weeks of work when he comes home), Chelsea offered transportation. Transportation solved the logistics of getting him home; it did nothing for the weeks of caregiving after. Chelsea reached for the nearest resource without checking whether it touched the real concern. The fiancée kept pushing — the behavioral signal that it hadn't.

In both cases: an answer or solution arrived before the concern was fully understood.

## What Chelsea can see in retrospect
She could name the fiancée's real concern clearly when asked: "She was concerned about the workload that was going to be put on her when he came home." That awareness was accessible. It just didn't arrive before she moved to a solution in the moment.
