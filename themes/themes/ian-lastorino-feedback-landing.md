# Delegation and feedback landing with direct reports

## The pattern
When Ian gives creative direction or feedback to direct reports, the deliverables come back not matching what he asked for. Rather than iterate with them, he ends up redoing the work himself to get it right. This repeats — it's not a one-off, it's a cycle.

## What Ian makes it mean
He interprets this as defiance or lack of effort: "they're not even trying to get what I'm asking for." Hard not to read it that way when it keeps happening.

## The hypothesis (coach's early read)
Two possible explanations:
1. They're hearing the feedback but missing it (skill/clarity gap)
2. The feedback itself isn't landing (communication method, framing, or alignment issue)

The coach suspects the *second* is more likely, based on the pattern. If it were a skill gap, it would show differently. The fact that it's creative direction specifically suggests the *handoff of direction* is the variable to explore.

## Observations
- **2026-06-17 — [intake](../sessions/ian-lastorino/2026-06-17-intake.md)** — Ian's natural response is to own the fix (high ownership, which is a strength) but this masks the root cause. Fixing the draft doesn't solve the feedback problem. Ian frames the situation as the team "not trying" or being defiant, rather than exploring whether the *communication* of direction is landing.
- **2026-06-17 — [follow-up: accountability and feedback integrity](../sessions/ian-lastorino/2026-06-17-follow-up.md)** — Ian is using *multiple modalities* of feedback (detailed write-ups, red-lined drafts, in-person conversations), which rules out "he's only using one channel." But feedback still isn't landing. Deeper: when Ian names that feedback wasn't incorporated, direct reports either make excuses or claim it's there when it isn't. This shifts the pattern from "communication gap" toward "accountability gap." Ian interprets this as dishonesty — not "we tried and fell short," but "we're managing you." Coach clarifies the specific gap: flat-out non-compliance vs. misinterpretation. Both suggest different root causes.
- **2026-06-17 — [deep dive: the what vs. the why in feedback](../sessions/ian-lastorino/2026-06-17-deep-dive-intent.md)** — Ian moves from "they're lying" to "they think they nailed it"—a genuine reframe. The core issue surfaces sharply: Ian is handing over the **what** (red-lines, corrections, specific fixes) but not the **why** (the underlying standard, the intent, what good looks like). His directs patch exactly what's marked and feel successful; Ian stares at the result thinking "that's not what I meant." When asked what they get in terms of the why, Ian says "basically nothing." This reframes the pattern entirely: not an accountability problem or a skill gap, but a **communication completeness problem**. The reasoning stays in Ian's head. Secondary question emerging: why does the why stay hidden? Time efficiency (red-lining is 2 min, explaining is 20), or an implicit assumption that at their level they should know the standard without being told?
- **2026-06-17 — [context vs. competence: the real assumption gap](../sessions/ian-lastorino/2026-06-17-stakeholder-context.md)** — Ian tests the assumption from the previous session and confirms: he *did* assume that at their level (decades of experience) they should understand the standard. Coach challenges this by pointing out that if the standard were truly obvious, it wouldn't miss as often. Ian then makes a critical clarification: "I understand what the final stakeholders would want." This reframes the entire gap: Ian isn't holding a universal craft standard—he's holding **stakeholder context**. Line of sight. His directs have general excellence but lack the specific knowledge of what stakeholders care about, what will land, what the winning move is. This context doesn't transfer through seniority; it transfers through conversation. The "should already know" assumption collapses into "they don't have information I have." This is the real lever: if Ian shares stakeholder context explicitly (not just corrections), deliverables land closer to the target.

## The root cause: what vs. why

**2026-06-17 — [follow-up](../sessions/ian-lastorino/2026-06-17-follow-up.md)** surfaced the **specific gap**: Ian delivers the **what** (detailed corrections, red-lines, specific fixes) but not the **why** (the underlying standard, the intent, what "good" looks like in context). This causes:

1. Directs patch exactly what's marked and feel they've succeeded
2. Ian's implicit standard (which he can see clearly) stays in his head
3. He stares at the result thinking "that's not what I meant"
4. They defend ("it's in there") because they genuinely believe they got it right

This reframes the pattern from "they're not trying" or "they're managing me" to **a communication completeness problem**. When Ian says "they get basically nothing" (referring to the why), he's identifying the exact gap.

The secondary question: Why does the why stay hidden? Time constraints (marking takes 2 min, explaining takes 20) or an implicit belief that at their level, they should already know the standard without being told?

- **2026-06-17 — [escalation and the buy-in gap](../sessions/ian-lastorino/2026-06-17-escalation-and-the-buy-in-gap.md)** — **Blindspot signal:** Under sustained pushback, Ian escalated from feedback to calling his direct report "lazy." The trigger was the direct report giving four objections (no time / takes too long / too much on my plate / those things don't matter). Ian treated all four as excuses and ran out of levers. Coach reframed: three objections are capacity complaints; the fourth is a buy-in gap — the direct report doesn't see the point of the change. These require different responses. Ian then admits his delegation request landed as "do it because I said so" rather than as a reasoned case. This connects directly to the what-vs-why gap: when Ian transmits the change without the stakeholder context behind it, authority is the only argument he has left. When authority fails under pushback, the escalation follows. Ian ends the session asking how to actually sell the why — a growth move.

## Next steps
- **Confirmed:** The why stays hidden because Ian assumes directs should already know — not because of time.
- **Reframed:** It's not a competence or standards issue; it's a **context issue**. Directs have craft but lack stakeholder knowledge.
- **Test:** In the next delegation cycle, hand over the stakeholder context explicitly. Name who cares about this, what they want, what constraints are real. Observe whether deliverables land closer and frustration decreases.
- **Longer play:** If sharing context shifts results, Ian has found his leverage point. The question then becomes: why wasn't he sharing this context before? Was it time? Assumption? Something else?
