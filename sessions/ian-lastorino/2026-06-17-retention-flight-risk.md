# 2026-06-17 — Retention: reading withdrawal signals in a direct report

## Context

Ian opens a new topic: a direct report named **Charles** whom he's identified as a flight risk. His stated goal is to figure out what to do to keep Charles.

## Signals Ian is reading

Ian lists specific behavioral shifts he's observing in Charles:
- Late to meetings
- Leaving on the dot (not staying a minute over)
- Coming in a few minutes late each day
- Not proactive on projects
- Terse in meetings

## Coach's reframe: withdrawal behaviors ≠ flight risk (necessarily)

The coach names what Ian is describing as **withdrawal behaviors** — and confirms Ian is reading them correctly. But then draws a critical distinction: withdrawal has more than one root cause. The same behavioral cluster can signal:

1. **Mental check-out / active job search** — the classic flight risk read
2. **Burnout** — exhaustion presenting as disengagement
3. **Personal/external stressor** — something heavy at home, bleeding into work
4. **Silent protest** — Charles is quietly pissed about something at work (passed over, project that went sideways, peer's departure dumping work on him, etc.) and is expressing it with behavior

The key coaching point: **the fix for each is completely different.** Pulling a retention lever (comp, title, a project) on the wrong diagnosis wastes goodwill and sometimes money. Before acting, Ian needs to know which root he's actually dealing with.

## Diagnostic question posed

The coach asks Ian: **Was this a gradual fade, or can you roughly point to when it changed?**

A switch that flipped on a specific week usually has an identifiable trigger. A gradual fade suggests slow burn or accumulation. This distinction narrows the hypothesis.

Session ends before Ian answers.

## Open thread

Ian needs to answer the fade-vs-flip diagnostic. That answer determines which conversation to have with Charles — and whether to have it as a curiosity check-in ("I've noticed X, how are you?") vs. a direct probe on a specific event ("since Y happened I've noticed...").
