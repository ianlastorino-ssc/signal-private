# 2026-06-17 — AI adoption: blank page, delegation training, and prompting

## Pivot from delegation work

Ian opens by asking where they left off; the coach picks up the feedback/delegation thread. Ian redirects: the org is pushing AI adoption and he doesn't know where to start. He asks for help navigating it.

## Scoping the ask

Coach clarifies: is this about Ian's own AI fluency, or about his role as L&D leader building training and rollout for the org?

Ian: "A little column A, a little column B. Let's focus on my AI use right now."

## Entry point: where the time goes

Coach affirms this order — hard to build training for a tool you don't actually live in. Asks where Ian's time goes in a typical week: what feels like a slog, what he puts off.

Ian: "churning out content is definitely slow."

Coach narrows: is it the blank-page start, or the back half — building, formatting, polishing once you know what you want to say?

Ian: "blank page starts."

## AI framing: the wall to bounce off

Coach explains the mechanic: AI's value here isn't being right or writing for you — it's giving you something to react to. Blank page is paralyzing because you're conjuring from nothing. Reacting to a mediocre draft is easy — you instantly know "no, not that, more like this." The move is "AI, give me a rough first cut so I'm editing instead of conjuring."

## Delegation training as test case

Coach asks: what's the next real piece of content on your plate?

Ian: "delegation training" — building training for new managers on how to properly delegate tasks and understand priority.

Coach notes the irony: Ian is building training on delegation, the same topic he's been working on with his own directs. "You're a little bit your own case study here."

## Prompting coaching: topic vs. problem

Coach explains the core prompting principle: generic prompt → generic output. The whole game is how much real context you hand AI upfront — not the topic, but the stuff only you know: who this is for, what failure mode you've actually watched happen, what good looks like in your org specifically.

Asks Ian to draft the opening prompt out loud.

Ian drafts: "I need a delegation training to teach new managers how to properly delegate tasks to take action, and understand priority. Outline it for me."

Coach gives the feedback directly: every word of that prompt is true for every company on earth. Nothing in it is Ian's. It describes the topic, not the problem. An AI handed that will return the generic five-box outline he could've Googled.

The tell: he described *what* (delegation), not the specific failure mode he's seen. Coach asks: when you watch your new managers try to delegate and it goes sideways, what does that actually look like?

Session ends with Ian asking for more prompting guidance.
