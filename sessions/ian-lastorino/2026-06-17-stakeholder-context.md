# 2026-06-17 — Context vs. competence: the real assumption gap

## Opening: sitting with the assumption

Coach asks Ian if he's had a chance to delegate since the last session, and whether he sat with the question: is the why staying hidden because of time, or because he assumes directs should already know the standard at their level?

Ian confirms: he assumed that at their level (decades of experience) they should understand it already.

## The reframe: context is not competence

Coach challenges the assumption head-on. If the standard were truly obvious to someone with their experience, it wouldn't miss as often as it does. So either it's not obvious to them, or it's obvious *to Ian* in a way it isn't to them.

Ian clarifies: "I understand what the final stakeholders would want."

**This is the crack.** What Ian is holding isn't a universal standard — it's **line of sight**. Context. He knows what stakeholders want because he's in rooms, conversations, relationships with them that his directs are not.

## The mechanism revealed

Someone with thirty years of experience can do genuinely excellent work and still miss, because they're aiming at "good in general" while Ian is aiming at "what this stakeholder will actually accept." Two different targets.

**The directs can't see Ian's target unless he points at it.**

This makes the "they should already know" assumption unfair — not because they're not skilled, but because Ian is holding a piece of the picture they were never given.

## The shift in frame

**Before:** "At their level, they should know the standard."
**Now:** "At their level, they have general craft. But they don't have the specific stakeholder context I have. That transfers only when I tell them."

This isn't insulting their seniority. It's acknowledging a different kind of knowledge — not competence, but access. Ian has it; they don't. Until he shares it, they're solving the wrong problem.

## Implication for the why

The "why" isn't the underlying craft principle (which they should have). The "why" is **the stakeholder context**: who's going to see this? What do they care about? What will they push back on? What's the winning move here?

That context doesn't transfer through seniority. It transfers through conversation.

## Pattern observation

- **Strength:** Ian sees stakeholder needs clearly; he's close to the stakeholders and has developed judgment about what will land
- **Blindspot:** Ian assumes this context is self-evident or should be inferred from craft alone, rather than explicitly handed over
- **Cost:** Directs deliver technically sound work that misses the specific target. Ian redoes it. The feedback cycle breaks.

## Coaching note

Ian is at an inflection point. He's moved from "they're not trying" → "they think they nailed it" → "they don't have the information I have." This last move is the one that opens the door to agency. It's not about them anymore. It's about *him choosing* to hand over the context he holds.

The question now is: Will he?

---

**Next:** Ian will likely run his next delegation cycle with this frame. The experiment: hand over the stakeholder context explicitly (not just the what, but the who-cares-and-why-they-care) and observe whether deliverables land more closely and frustration drops.
