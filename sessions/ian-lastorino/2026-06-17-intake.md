# 2026-06-17 — Intake: delegation and feedback with direct reports

## Opening signal
Ian opens with: "I am having some trouble with my direct reports."

## The core situation
When drafts/deliverables come back from direct reports, they don't match Ian's direction or feedback. Rather than iterate with them, Ian ends up redoing the work himself to get it right. This is a pattern: it happens repeatedly, and Ian interprets it as the team not trying to meet his direction — almost like defiance.

## Coach's hypothesis (to explore)
Two possible interpretations of why feedback isn't landing:
1. They're hearing the feedback but missing it (skill gap, clarity gap)
2. The feedback itself isn't landing in the first place (communication method, framing, or alignment issue)

## Method of communication (to clarify next turn)
Coach began exploring HOW direction is being communicated: in-room discussion, notes on drafts, Slack messages, docs, etc. This is a critical distinction — the medium affects whether creative direction lands.

## Observations
- When the work comes back wrong, Ian's natural move is to fix it (owns the outcome)
- This pattern repeats, creating a cycle where Ian is the de facto executor
- Ian doesn't yet see the alternative: that fixing the draft doesn't solve the feedback problem, and it's back again next time
- Coach is setting up the hypothesis that the *communication* of direction may be the leverage point, not the person's intent or capability

## Open questions for next session
- How is creative direction actually being handed off? (sync vs async, visual vs verbal, etc.)
- Are the direct reports getting the *reasoning* behind the direction, or just the final form?
- Is there a difference between how Ian communicates direction vs how they're interpreting it?
- What would it look like if they *understood* the direction but chose differently?

## Second exchange — frustration and multiple feedback methods
Ian comes back and expresses frustration explicitly: "yes, i am frustrated." He wants to be clear that he DOES delegate and give feedback well. He's sent detailed write-ups, applied red-lines to drafts, and given in-person feedback.

This is important: Ian is using *multiple modalities* of feedback. He's not just assuming one channel works. He's trying different approaches — written detail, visual markup, synchronous conversation.

And still, the feedback isn't landing.

Coach asks: "When you've handed back the red-lined doc, or you've had the in-person sit-down — what do they actually do next? Not what you hoped. What you've actually watched happen."

**Signal:** Ian's frustration is real, and it points to something he hasn't yet articulated: that trying *harder* at feedback delivery (more modalities, more detail, more personal) isn't the same as having the feedback *land*. The coach is pushing him toward observing what actually happens after the feedback, not what he intended by giving it.
