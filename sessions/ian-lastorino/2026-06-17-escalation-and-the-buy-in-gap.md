# 2026-06-17 — Escalation and the buy-in gap

## The opening: feedback went badly

Ian comes in with a report: he gave a direct report feedback on the delegation issue and it escalated. He told the direct report to "stop being lazy" and just follow his changes.

## What set it off

The direct report pushed back with multiple objections:
- "I don't have time to do it that way"
- "It takes too long"
- "I have too much on my plate"
- "Those things don't matter"

Ian read all four as excuses. Under that framing, sustained excuse-making tipped him from giving feedback into naming it as laziness.

## The coach's reframe: two different complaints

Not all four objections are the same. Three of them — time, effort, workload — are "I'm drowning." The fourth — "those things don't matter" — is "I don't buy into why you're asking for this."

These require different responses. The capacity complaints can potentially be worked with (workload, prioritization). The fourth is a buy-in problem: the direct report doesn't see the point of the change.

## The critical admission

Coach asks: did Ian actually sell them on the why, or did it land as "do it because I said so"?

Ian acknowledges: the latter. His delegation request came as authority, not as reasoning.

This directly connects to the prior thread — Ian holds stakeholder context his directs don't have, and he's been transmitting the *what* (the specific change) without the *why* (why the stakeholder cares, what goes wrong if it doesn't happen, who feels it).

## The move Ian is asking for

Ian ends the session asking how to "do the former" — how to actually sell the why rather than invoking authority. This is a growth move: he's past the event and asking for the tool.

---

**Coaching note:** The escalation to "lazy" is a signal about what happens when Ian's authority-based feedback loop fails under sustained resistance. When the direct report won't comply and Ian hasn't built a why-based case, the only lever left is frustration. Ian is not yet aware this is a structural problem, not just a bad moment.
