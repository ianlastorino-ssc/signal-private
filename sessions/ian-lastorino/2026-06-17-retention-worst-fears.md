# 2026-06-17 — Retention: worst fears named, management style on the table

## Context

Continuation of the Charles retention thread. The previous session ended with an open
question: if you sat down with Charles deliberately, what specific things would you point
to?

## What happened

Ian answered the open question: he'd point to the behavioral evidence he'd described
("the late starts, leaving on the dot, going quiet") and frame it explicitly as non-
confrontational — "it's not an attack, it's just not like him, and I want to know what's
going on so I can help." This is a real opening — it names observations without
attributing motive, and it gives Charles an invitation rather than a corner.

Coach validated the approach and asked the next diagnostic: what's the answer you'd
LEAST want to hear?

Ian deflected — he pivoted to asking for generic employee retention best practices. The
coach named directly why it wouldn't land: generic retention tactics fail when they
don't match the actual root cause, and applying the wrong lever to Charles could make
him feel more unseen, not less. Coach shared the five categories of why good people
actually leave (pay, feeling unseen, no clear path forward, work no longer fits, not
enough autonomy) as a *listening map* — not a menu of actions, but a way to categorize
whatever Charles says.

Coach returned to the original question: which of these do you suspect it is, and which
would you least want it to be?

Ian answered directly: worst case is Charles doesn't say anything at all. Beyond that —
that he dislikes working at the company, or dislikes Ian's management style specifically.

Coach named the reframe: the answer Ian fears most (his own management style) is the
one he can actually do something about. The company-level answer is largely out of his
hands; the management-style answer is his to change if he hears it.

Session ended with: if that's what comes out, how do you want to handle it in the moment
so Charles doesn't clam back up to "fine"? Ian has not yet answered.

## Signals

- Ian can now articulate a non-confrontational, observation-based opening for the Charles
  conversation — a real capability shift from passive hallway check-ins.
- Ian deflects to generic tactics when asked to sit with an uncomfortable self-diagnostic
  question (fear of being the problem). This is avoidance, not confusion.
- Ian named fear of his own management style without prompting — shows self-awareness
  and willingness to go there when asked directly.
- The conversation is approaching the point where Ian will need to decide whether to
  actually have it with Charles.

## Open thread

If Charles comes back with management-style feedback, Ian hasn't yet worked out how to
stay open and not defensive in that moment. Resume here: how does he want to handle it
so the conversation doesn't close back down?
