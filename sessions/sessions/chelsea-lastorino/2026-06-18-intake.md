# 2026-06-18 — Intake: de-escalation and the convince trap

## Opening signal
Chelsea opens: "My name is Chelsea Lastorino, I am a social worker for a hospital. I had a situation at work that I want to talk through regarding de-escalation with a patient."

## The core situation
A patient's fiancée was disgruntled about the patient's discharge. Chelsea's task was to communicate why the discharge was appropriate. Her framing going in: convince them. This led to raised tensions rather than resolution.

## Coach's early read
The word "convince" is the diagnostic signal. Walking into an emotionally charged conversation with a predetermined outcome — get them to accept the discharge — tends to produce resistance rather than reduce it. The fiancée likely had specific concerns Chelsea hadn't surfaced yet. Chelsea was trying to land a conclusion before understanding the question.

## What the fiancée was actually worried about
Chelsea answered: the fiancée's real concern was the **workload** — not the discharge itself, but the weeks of caregiving waiting for her when he came home.

## Chelsea's response in the situation
She acknowledged it was stressful, offered transportation home to reduce the burden, then explained the medical reasons for discharge. Two separate problems being addressed: logistics (transportation) and medical clearance. Neither touched the caregiver burden the fiancée named.

## The mismatch
Transportation solves the drive home. The fiancée named the weeks after. Chelsea reached for the nearest available resource, but it addressed a problem the fiancée hadn't raised. The fiancée kept pushing — which is the signal that the real concern still hadn't been reached.

## Open question (unanswered at session end)
Coach asked: put yourself back in that moment — what do you think she actually needed from you right then?

## Observations
- Chelsea framed the challenge as "convince" — task-focused, outcome-led entry into conflict.
- Chelsea could name the fiancée's real concern in retrospect (workload/caregiving), but her intervention missed it in the moment.
- Solution offered (transportation) addressed logistics, not the caregiver fear. Classic mismatch: solving a problem that wasn't raised.
- The fiancée's continued pushing after the solution offer is the behavioral signal that the real concern hadn't been touched.
- Both moves (convince framing + wrong-level solution) share the same structure: arriving with an answer before the underlying concern is fully understood.
