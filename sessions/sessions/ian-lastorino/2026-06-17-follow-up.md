# 2026-06-17 — Follow-up: Accountability and feedback integrity

## The reframe: from dishonesty to intent gap

Ian came frustrated: he's giving detailed feedback (write-ups, red-lines, in-person conversations), but it isn't landing. When he checks the work, his feedback isn't applied. When he asks why, they claim it's there — basically lying.

The coach helped him reframe this quickly:
- Started: "They're lying"
- Landed: "They think they nailed it" — they genuinely believe they gave him what he asked for

The gap isn't character; it's **intent**. Ian can see the standard clearly (the why), but he's handing over only the what (the red-line, the fix). The reasoning stays in his head.

## The core pattern: what vs. why

Ian gives:
- The what: "change this, fix that" (red-lines in documents, specific corrections)
- What they don't get: the why — the underlying standard, the intent, what "good" actually means in context

When directs patch exactly what's marked, they feel they've won. Ian stares at it and thinks "that's not what I meant."

Ian admits: "They get basically nothing" (in terms of the why).

## The second-order question: why the why stays hidden

The coach probes: Is it time efficiency (marking takes 2 min, explaining takes 20), or is it an implicit expectation — **at their level, shouldn't they already know this without spelling it out?**

This is where the real barrier may live: a belief that explaining the standard feels like you're insulting their seniority or capability, so it gets skipped.

---

## Observable pattern
- Detailed feedback delivery (strength)
- Clarity about standards in Ian's own head (clarity for him, not for them)
- Assumption that the why should be obvious (potential blindspot or unexamined expectation)
- Frustration when intent doesn't transfer (signal of the gap, not dishonesty from directs)

## Next
- Ian to sit with: is it time, or is it a belief about what they "should" know?
- This will inform whether the move is to allocate more time for explanatory feedback, or to examine assumptions about their readiness/seniority/responsibility.
