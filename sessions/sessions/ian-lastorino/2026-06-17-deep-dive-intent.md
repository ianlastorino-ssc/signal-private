# 2026-06-17 — Deep dive: the what vs. the why in feedback

## Resumption and reframing

Ian returns mid-afternoon and wants to keep working on the delegation piece. Coach quickly picks up context and takes him deeper into the pattern.

## The core insight: feedback as incomplete handoff

Ian has been giving feedback in multiple formats — detailed write-ups, red-lined docs, in-person sit-downs. That's generous. But the directs are returning work that doesn't match what he asked for. When he names it, they either give excuses or claim it's already there (which Ian initially reads as dishonesty, then corrects to "they imagine it's right").

**Coach's clarification:** The gap isn't honesty — it's intent. Ian can see the standard clearly in his own head. But what he hands over is the **what** (the correction, the fix, the red-line). The **why** — the underlying standard, the intent, what good actually looks like — stays with him.

## The specific mechanism

**Ian's process:**
- Marks up the doc: "change this, fix that"
- Sits down in person
- Hands it back

**What happens next:**
- Directs patch exactly what's marked
- Feel good about it (they did the thing)
- Ian stares at it: "that's not what I meant"
- They defend: "it's in there" (genuinely believing they succeeded)

**Ian's admission:** When asked what the why is, "They get basically nothing."

This is the core gap. Not a skill problem. Not dishonesty. A **communication completeness problem**: the reasoning behind the correction never left Ian's head.

## The hypothesis beneath it

Coach probes why the why stays hidden. Two possibilities:

1. **Time pressure.** Red-lining is fast (2 minutes). Explaining the standard takes 20. Given a full plate, Ian opts for speed, assuming the why is obvious.

2. **An unexamined assumption.** At their level, shouldn't they already know the standard? Explaining it might feel like you're insulting their capability, so it gets skipped.

Coach doesn't push for an answer yet — just plants the question. This is the inflection point where Ian's next move will clarify which barrier is real.

## Pattern recognition

- **Strength:** Ian gives feedback generously across multiple modalities
- **Clarity gap:** The standard is clear to Ian; it's not clear to them
- **Potential blindspot:** Ian may assume directs should infer the why without it being stated explicitly
- **Emotion signal:** Frustration turning toward distrust; framing it as "they're not trying" when it might be "I haven't handed over the whole picture"

## Next

Ian to sit with the second-order question: **Is the why staying hidden because it takes time, or because part of you expects them to already know it?**

This will determine the intervention. If time: allocate more time for explanatory feedback. If assumption: examine whether you're holding back the reasoning because you expect them to read your mind, and what that costs.

---

**Coach's observation:** Ian is close to a real shift here. The move from "they're lying" to "they think they nailed it" was quick and genuine. He's ready to own the communication gap instead of blaming their effort or character. The next turn is whether he can sit with the discomfort of being more explicit about standards — and whether that's a time issue or a belief issue.
