# 2026-06-17 — Managing up: evidence brought, still rejected

## Continuation

Picks up from [2026-06-17 — managing up: diagnostic rephrased](2026-06-17-managing-up-rephrased.md). The diagnostic question — "know something they don't" vs. "would just make a different call" — had been posed in its plainest form and was unanswered.

## New ground this exchange

Ian answers the fork: he wants to **change the direction** (confirmed, not lead through it).

The coach then surfaces the information-vs-position distinction: what moves a decision from above is new information, not another opinion. Leadership already made a call; another view is friction. The leverage Ian has is his vantage point on how the direction actually breaks at the ground level.

**Critical new signal:** Before the diagnostic could be fully explored, Ian reveals he has already tried. He raised his concerns with leadership and was shut down. The coach asks whether he put a *specific consequence to something leadership is on the hook for* — or just his read that the direction is wrong. Ian's answer:

> "No even with evidence."

Ian believes he brought concrete evidence and was still told no.

## What this changes

This shifts the coaching territory significantly. The two remaining possibilities:

1. **The evidence was real but not framed around their outcomes.** Leadership may have heard it as Ian's concern, not a threat to something *they* care about. The framing gap — "here's the cost to you specifically" vs. "here's why I think this is wrong" — is a different skill, and is still worth testing.
2. **The direction is genuinely decided at a level above the conversation Ian is having.** Some decisions are made for political, strategic, or structural reasons that aren't visible to Ian and won't be moved at his level. Knowing which case you're in saves capital.

Session ends here — coach's question about *what exactly* Ian put in front of them is unanswered. The answer is needed to distinguish these two cases.

## Open thread

What did Ian actually bring as evidence? Was it framed around a cost *to leadership's outcomes* — something they're personally accountable for — or was it framed around why the direction is wrong in general? The distinction determines whether there's still a live influence play or whether the situation calls for a different kind of navigation.
