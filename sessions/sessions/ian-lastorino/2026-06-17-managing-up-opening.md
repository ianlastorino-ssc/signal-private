# 2026-06-17 — Managing up: opening the question

## The pivot

Ian opens a new area: "I feel like I need support managing up." This is a distinct direction from all prior sessions — previously focused on his relationship with direct reports; now pointing upward toward his own leadership.

## What he's actually after

Ian specifies: he wants to know what to do when he doesn't agree on company direction — specifically the direction set for his department and the tasks assigned to it. He notes these are delegated from above with limited say at his level.

## The fork

Coach surfaces a choice:
- **Change the direction** — influence upward, move the decision
- **Lead the team well through it** — maintain integrity downward, carry something you didn't choose without faking enthusiasm or poisoning it for the team

Ian chooses: **change the direction**.

## The coaching move

Coach reframes what actually works when trying to move a decision from above: not leading with position or disagreement (another opinion is just friction to people who already weighed it), but **with information they don't have**. The key vantage point Ian holds — that leadership doesn't — is ground-level visibility into how the direction actually breaks, what it costs, where the risk shows up.

Coach surfaces the critical diagnostic question (session ends here, Ian hasn't answered yet):

> Is Ian seeing something leadership *can't* see from their altitude — a real cost, a risk, a way this breaks on the ground? Or would he weigh the *same facts* differently if he were at their level?

**The stakes:** The first (new information) is genuinely winnable upward. The second (different values or weighting) almost never is — and spending political capital on an unwinnable case is its own cost.

---

**Coaching note:** Session ended before Ian answered. The answer to this question determines whether the next move is a real influence campaign or a values conversation (or acceptance). Both are worth coaching through — but they are different work.
